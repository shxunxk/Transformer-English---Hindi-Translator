AUTOMATIC ELIMINATION OF LEXICAL AMBIGUITIES IN MODERN GREEK: PRESENTATION OF THE ELAG SYSTEM

Résumé
        Notre étude s'inscrit dans le cadre de l'ensemble des travaux, entrepris en collaboration entre les laboratoires de Linguistique Informatique de l'Université Aristote de Thessaloniki et de l'Université de Marne-la-Vallée, visant à une description complète et formalisée de la langue grecque. Nous présentons le système d'élimination d'ambiguïtés lexicales Elag (Laporte et al. 1999) récemment mis à jour et intégré à l'environnement d'analyse automatique de textes Unitex. Le programme procède à la levée d'ambiguïtés morpho-syntaxiques sur l'automate du texte en utilisant des grammaires locales, qui imposent des contraintes grammaticales sur les mots en fonction de leur contexte d'apparition dans les textes. Initialement développé pour le français, le programme a été adapté pour pouvoir être utilisé avec l'ensemble des langues traitées dans Unitex (anglais, grec, italien, norvégien, portugais, russe, espagnol, thaï). 
       Nous exposons le formalisme permettant d'écrire les contraintes grammaticales utilisées par Elag, à l'aide de graphes Unitex et nous décrivons les propriétés de ces grammaires de désambiguïsation. Dans un second temps, nous présentons les grammaires que nous avons écrites pour lever les ambiguïtés dans les textes grecs,  ainsi que les résultats que nous avons obtenus jusqu'à présent. Nous nous sommes essentiellement intéressés aux  ambiguïtés morphologiques notamment les ambiguïtés de cas.  A titre d’exemple, la forme βιβλίο (livre) est en même temps nominatif, accusatif et vocatif du singulier.  Aussi, sa forme au pluriel βιβλία (livres) est à la fois nominatif, accusatif et vocatif. Nous montrons comment certaines grammaires Elag permettent de résoudre automatiquement un grand nombre de ces ambiguïtés.
       

1. Introduction

       One of the major issues in text processing is dealing with lexical ambiguities of natural languages. This problem becomes more important when using large dictionaries with fine-grained tagset. Indeed the dictionary we elaborated at the university of Thessaloniki contains more than one million words tagged with morphological and syntactic information and its application on greek corpus1 gives an average ambiguity rate of more than two tags per word, which leads to a combination of a huge number of lexical analyses for the text (in the order of thousands lexical interpretations per sentence). All these analyses create a lot of noise that needs to be considered on further steps of text processing (like syntactic parsing) and greatly increases their computational complexity.
	This article is about the Elag developed at I.G.M. (Institut Gaspard Monge. Université de Marne-la-Valléedisambiguation system and its use to disambiguate text written in Modern Greek. Our program processes to the partial lexical disambiguation of text corpus with the use of hand-written linguistic descriptions specifying constraints on words depending on their syntactic context. After describing the program and the formalism of disambiguation grammars, we present a collection of Elag grammars we created in order to disambiguate greek texts. This work is mostly based on the study of lexical ambiguities in Modern Greek elaborated by T. Kyriacopoulou (2003) and the local grammars she wrote in order to locate such ambiguities in corpus. We present an evaluation of the application of these grammars on electronic corpus, and we conclude by giving some perspectives.     

2. Elag System

       In most NLP (Natural Language Processing) systems, lexical ambiguity is dealt with stochastic approaches, which present their result as a text totally tagged. Those POS (Part Of Speech) taggers use some statistic computations (Dermatas, 1995), sometimes combined with linguistic data (Brill, 1995), to determine for each word its most probable syntactic tag. These methods usually achieve good results, but tagging errors are unavoidable with these stochastic models, which fail to catch natural language complexity. Indeed, the most precise taggers hardly reach the bound of 4% of mistakes, which results in almost one word per sentence wrongly tagged. Moreover, the tagsets used in such systems are usually small and contain very little linguistic information; for instance the widely used Penn TreeBank tagset (Marcus and al., 1993), which have only 36 different tags for English. Such methods do not scale well with larger tagsets containing precise morphological and syntactic informations.
	Elag is a text disambiguation system initially created by E. Laporte (1999) to process French written texts tagged with the DELAF dictionary (Courtois et al., 1990). We recently redesigned the program (Blanc et al., 2004) so that it can potentially be used to disambiguate texts in any language supported by the corpus processing system Unitex2. The program disambiguates the text automaton, that is a text representation obtained after dictionary lookup, which permits to present its ambiguity in a natural and graphical way. Figure 1 presents the text automaton for the sentence Το νησί χαρακτηρίζεται από την ήπια ανάπτυξη (The island is characterised by a bland development). 


Figure 1: Text Automaton

	Each path from the leftmost box to the rightmost one gives a hypothetical lexical analysis for the sentence. Elag uses hand-written disambiguation grammars to discard paths that don't verify some specified morpho-syntactic constraints. The result of this operation is a partially disambiguated text automaton, such as in the disambiguation system presented by Koskenniemi (1990).
	An Elag grammar is made of two parts: the if part and the then part. The if part, which is delimited by two <!> symbols, specifies the context where the grammar will be applied. The then part consists in one or more patterns delimited by the <=> symbol and describes some constraints that should be verified by the words in the sequences matching with the if part. If a path in the text automaton matches with the pattern described in the if part of the grammar, then this sequence should also match with one of the constraints patterns, otherwise the sequence is said to be rejected by the grammar and it is removed from the text automaton. Figure 2 presents a simple Elag grammar.

Figure 2: Simple Elag Grammar

	In the if part, we describe a simple noun phrase beginning with a determiner in accusative (denoted by the code A); in the then part, we force the noun and its possible proceeding adjectives to be in accusative case also. The purpose of the middle symbols <!> and <=> is to synchronize between the two sequences. The result of the application of this grammar to our sentence is shown in Figure 3. We can see for example that nominative and vocative codes have been correctly deleted from the sequence την.DET ήπια.A ανάπτυξη.N (the.DET bland.A development.N). Concurrent paths are kept in the automaton as they are not considered by our grammar, but they can still be managed with other ones. This way, with the accumulation of disambiguation grammars, the program filters the text automaton, eliminating as much wrong analyses as possible (but still keeping unresolved ambiguities) in order to ease the task of further text processing, such as syntactic parsing.

3. Application in Modern Greek
 
       This application of the Elag system concerns the elimination of lexical ambiguities in Modern Greek. A great part of them are detected in all four cases (nominative, genitive, accusative, vocative) of nouns and adjectives, as well as in ambiguities among determiners, personal and possessive pronouns. These types of ambiguities can be solved by grammars integrated in systems of automatic text analysis of in real time. The starting point of this research was the local grammars created by Tita Kyriacopoulou (2003), which were transformed into the Elag formalism and were enriched. The Elag formalism allows the simultaneous application of a set of grammars contrary to local grammars that can be applied only one at a time. 
	In the lexical analysis, words are often ambiguous when not in context. On the contrary, the morphological ambiguities are reduced to a great extent when elaborated in context (T. Kyriacopoulou, 2003). Therefore, the study was based on the definition of potential context of the most common ambiguous parts of speech. That context, in this research, was represented by graphs. 

3.1. The nominative case 
	After having checked out the nouns and adjectives of all declensions in Modern Greek (D. Holton, P. Mackridge, E. Filippaki-Warburton, 2002), we noted the existing ambiguities. In order to achieve a complete linguistic analysis, we took into consideration even the exceptions of each declension, as they may occur in corpora. The examples below are the ones best pointing out ambiguity problems.

	In the following graph4, the case of nominative is described by the definition of a potential context. In particular, we have grouped some indicative linking verbs (αναγορεύομαι: nominate/acclaim διατελώ: be/remain etc.) requiring exclusively a predicative in nominative. Between these verbs and the predicative, may occur a preposition (από:from) and/or a determiner (ένας:a/an). Finally, the second part has been divided into three parts in order to eliminate the potential ambiguities existing between adjectives and common forms in the masculine and feminine gender.

4. Conclusions – Perspectives 

	As the Elag system was created in 2003, this study was the first attempt to represent greek grammar rules in order to eliminate ambiguities from electronic corpora.  Greek grammar rules already existed (A. Anastassiadis-Symeonidis, T. Kyriakopoulou, E. Sklavounou, I. Thilikos, R. Voskaki, 2000; T. Kyriacopoulou, 2003) in the form of local grammars, which located the ambiguities without integrating the changes in the text. These grammars were, however, elaborated and transformed into the Elag formalism. 
       The priority of this initial research was to set the syntactic limits of cases in Modern Greek.  Our intention was not to solve entirely the problem of ambiguities, as it is too complicated. On the one hand, the created grammars need to be improved by adding more syntactic constraints. On the other hand, new grammars should be created to describe, as much as possible, the use of cases in terms of context.  All these grammars need to be constantly tested in large corpora. Finally, the ultimate purpose of this study is to achieve, in the long term, a greek corpus semi-automatically labelled.

1. Introduction
      This article is a research in the field of computational linguistics and deals with eliminating ambiguities in a semi-automatic way. In Modern Greek, at the morphological level, there are two different kinds of lexical ambiguities: firstly, the same form may represent more than one different inflected forms (e.g. 1a, 1b) and secondly the same form can correspond to more than one different grammatical categories (e.g. 2a, 2b). More specifically, in the example 1a, the nominal form κλειδί corresponds to the nominative, accusative and vocative cases. In the example 1b the verbal form είναι corresponds to the third person of the present tense of the singular as well as of the plural number. In the example 2a, the form το can be a determiner or a personal pronoun, and in the example 2b, the form ήπια can be a verb, an adjective or an adverb.

      This research concerns the elimination of ambiguities among different cases of nouns and adjectives as well as ambiguities between determiners and pronouns. After mentioning previous studies on semi-automatic ambiguity elimination and after describing how the Elag programme functions, we will describe in detail some Elag grammars created.
2. Construction of disambiguation grammars
2.1. Previous studies on disambiguation grammars
      Anastassiadis-Symeonidis, Kyriakopoulou, Sklavounou, Thilikos and Voskaki (2000), Sklavounou (1995), Bakoura and Voskaki (2004), Kazantzi and Voskaki (2004) and Thilikos and Voskaki (2003) have already produced studies on semi-automatical ambiguity elimination in Modern Greek. The first studies on the processed ambiguities have been conducted by Kyriacopoulou (2005) and Thilikos (2000). A semi-automatic elimination of ambiguities is based on computational linguistics rules, which are often different from the linguistics ones. Namely, linguistics rules must be transformed into computational linguistics ones with a strictly formalised structure. Such rules are applied on texts by corpus processing systems.
      Initially, the disambiguation rules, in terms of computational linguistics, were represented by local grammars. Local grammars are graphs that eliminate ambiguities by determining constraints in terms of context. The disadvantage of these grammars is that they are applied partially on the processed text and the applied corrections are not permanent on the text automaton. On the contrary, graphs in the Elag formalism are applied on the entire ambiguous text and the changes are kept permanently. The text automaton concerns the full morphological description of the entire text sentence per sentence. That is, for every ambiguous lemma all potential different labels are represented in detail.
      Thus, researchers, such as Laporte (2001), pointed out the need of creating a system that would apply a grammar to a text, in the sense that ambiguities would be actually removed. The materialisation of this idea is the Elag system, which will be presented below.
2.2. The Elag programme
      The Elag programme, inspired by E. Laporte (E. Laporte & A. Monceaux:1999) and created by O. Blanc, is integrated in the corpus processing system called Unitex5, which was created by S. Paumier (Assistant professor, Université de Marne-la-Vallée). Unitex, when processing a text, applies electronic monolingual dictionaries6 containing the morphological description7 of each word. Every word may have a lot of different labels among which only one is correct in the specific context. When lexical ambiguities are to be eliminated, the text is shown in the form of a finite-state transducer called text automaton (figure 1), which allows to present ambiguities in a graphical way. 
      

      Figure 1: Text automaton (e.g. Yesterday, the minister asked for him.)
      
      As shown in figure 1, below the text, there are two windows for text automata: the first one represents the text automaton before the grammar(s) application by Elag and the second one represents the text automaton the grammar application. Elag applies one rule at a time, which can consist of a set of grammars. Figure 2 shows the formalism of an Elag grammar with no linguistic information.


      Figure 2: Elag grammar with no linguistic information
      
      Each grammar has two rows8: the “if part” and the “then part”. The “if part”, delimited by two <!>, describes the context which must be located in order to apply the grammar. The “then part”, having two or, rarely, three rows and delimited by two <=>, specifies the same context in order to limit the different characterisations of a form. Different characterisations are shown in the automaton as different paths. In order to read easily an Elag grammar, we could translate the upper row as “If you find the sequence X” and the lower row as “then, this sequence is X1…” or as “then keep only the description(s) that agree(s) with the sequence X1”. All grammars that will be described in the next chapter, as well as 30% of the dictionary used in our research, are available for free on the Internet9 when downloading Unitex, in a file called “Elag”. 
      An Elag rule has certain significant advantages: firstly, an Elag rule, when applied, does not only locate the sequence described, but also interferes to the morphological analysis of the text. That is, changes are saved to the text after grammars are applied. Secondly, the applied Elag rule can consist of more than one grammars, each concerning different type of ambiguity. These grammars are applied at the same time as one united rule. Last but not least, the user can compare the linguistic analysis of the text before and after the grammar application.
2.3. Elag grammars
      This research follows a study produced last year on the creation of Elag disambiguation grammars for Modern Greek. (Blanc, Ioannidou, Voskaki:2005). Since then, research went on, grammars were applied to an extended corpus and they were enriched. In total, the grammars presented below are applied to the electronic version of the Greek newspaper TA NEA (1997-2003), offered to us by C. Fairon (Université Catholique de Louvain) as well as to Greek corpora appeared on the Internet by 2001, offered to us by F. Guenthner (Center for Information & Language Processing, Ludwig-Maximilian Universität München). The enriched grammars we present in the next chapter concern ambiguities between determiners and pronouns and others concern the nominative, the genitive and the accusative case.
2.3.1. Ambiguities between determiners and pronouns
      Ambiguities between determiners and pronouns may be found in the following forms: το, του, της, τον, την, τους, τις, τα. Here are some examples:
      
         3. ΤΟ πρόγραμμα έχει ως στόχο να προβάλλει την πολιτιστική κληρονομιά της κάθε χώρας
(THE programme aims to promote the inheritance of every country10) To   DET+Def:Nns11

         4. Του αποκάλυψε ΤΟ λόγο για τον οποίον ήρθε
         (He revealed to him THE reason for which he came)
         Το   DET+Def:Ams

5. Η κοινοτική χρηματοδοτική στήριξη μπορεί να φτάσει μέχρι το 50% των συνολικών δαπανών για ΤΟ έργο και δεν μπορεί να υπερβεί το ποσό των 250.000 ECU
(The Community financial support can reach (THE) 50% of the total expenses for the project and cannot exceed the 250.000 ECU)
         Το   DET+Def:Ans

         6. Η κυβέρνηση ΤΟ ζήτησε
         (The governement asked for IT)
         Το   PRO+Pers:Ans 
         
      One way to distinguish a determiner from a pronoun is to define which parts of speech follow a determiner. Indeed, before an adjective, a noun, a numerical determiner or a number, we can have a determiner; on the contrary we cannot have a pronoun other than a personal one (e.g.7). 
      
         7. Είναι ΤΑ κορδόνια ΤΩΝ ωραίων ΤΟΥ παπουτσιών
         (They are THE laces of HIS nice shoes)
         Του   PRO+Pers:Gms
      
      Thus, a grammar we have created: “If you find a pronoun preceding an adjective, a noun, a numerical determiner or a number, then if it is a pronoun, it can only be a personal one.” Thus, if it is not a personal pronoun, it is a determiner. In order to improve this graph, we have added the symbol V:K which represents the participle having always the syntactic functions of a noun (figure 3) (e.g. 8)
      
         8. Είναι ο οδηγός ΤΟΥ χτυπημένου αυτοκινήτου
         (He’s the driver of THE crashed car)
         Του    DET+Def:Gns
      

	Figure 3: Elag grammar eliminating ambiguities between determiners and pronouns (1).

	In addition to this, we have produced another graph (figure 4) completing the previous one. This graph eliminates all determiners, apart from the numerical ones when preceding a verb. According to this grammar, when the system finds either a determiner, a preposition or a pronoun before a verb in all tenses and modes (all three grammatical categories will be recognised), this form preceding the verb can only be either a pronoun or a numerical determiner.
 

	Figure 4: Elag grammar eliminating ambiguities between determiners and pronouns (2).

2.3.2. The nominative case
      Ambiguities between nominative and other cases are detected in all genders. Here are some representative examples of every genre:
         
         9. γίγαντες		N:Nmp:Amp:Vmp (giants)
         
         10. γυναίκα	N:Nfs:Afs:Vfs (woman)
         
	11. τραπέζι		Nns:Ans:Vns (table)
       
      In the graph created initially concerning the nominative case, we were describing the syntax of some linking verbs (γίνομαι: become, βαφτίζομαι: be baptised) requiring a predicative in nominative. In the graph below, we removed these verbs, because the syntax of every verb is described in lexicon-grammars, another function of Unitex, which attributes syntactic information to words which are related to verbs12. That is, in lexicon-grammars, the predicative of a verb is recognized and this information can be used, in the future, to eliminate ambiguities that are detected in subjects, predicatives or objects of verbs.
      Therefore, the nominative Elag grammar is limited to defining the nominative case of nominal groups by the article in nominative. Namely, since the masculine or the feminine article in nominative is never ambiguous, we use it to eliminate the genitive, accusative and vocative cases of the following noun, adjective etc.
      In order to improve the applicability percentage of the grammar created, we removed most of the “if part” apart from the determiner. The increase of the applicability percentage is achieved, because when there is less information in the “if part”, there are less constraints in the context, and as a consequence, the system locates more sequences that correspond to the “if part” and it applies the grammar to more sentences. More specifically, the grammar described the following: “When you find a determiner in nominative and a nominal group (“if part”), the nominal group is a nominative (“then part”). On the contrary, the grammar in figure 6 defines: “When you find a determiner (“if part”), this determiner is either in genitive, either in accusative, or it is in nominative and the following nominal group is also in nominative.”
      Finally, we have enriched the nominative graph, in the sense that the description in the “then part” is more detailed as grammars were applied to a larger corpus and we came across more different grammatical categories that followed a determiner in nominative. The letter <E> in a box means that its content is optional.
      In the following text automaton (figure 7), one can see the results of the Elag grammar for the nominative case. In particular, when the form η is a determiner, then the adjective that follows is in nominative. When it is an abbreviation or a conjunction, the grammar is not applied and no ambiguity is eliminated.

2.3.3. The genitive case
      For most nouns, the genitive case is not ambiguous with the nominative, accusative or vocative cases. The indeclinable nouns and the masculine nouns ending in –ας are the only exception to this (e.g.12). The Elag grammar for genitive is, thus, created for these exceptions. The ambiguities are eliminated, for once more, describing some constraints in terms of context. The context requiring genitive is limited to some prepositions (λόγω: because of, μεταξύ:among/between, μέσω:by/through) (e.g. 13). 
      
         12. ασανσέρ	N:Nns:Gns:Ans:Vns:Nnp:Gnp:Anp:Vnp (elevator)
         
         13. Ανέβηκαν με τις σκάλες λόγω χαλασμένου ΑΣΑΝΣΕΡ
         (They went up by the stairs because of a broken ELEVATOR)
         ασανσέρ   N:Gns
      
      Similarly to the nominative graph, in order to increase the applicability of the genitive grammar (figure 8), we removed most of the “if part” apart from all the prepositions requiring a genitive. The best applicability percentage is achieved, because there is less information in the “if part” and the grammar is applied to more sentences.
      Moreover, we added another nominal group in genitive because it is often noticed in texts. The nominal groups are presented in a linear succession so as to be more legible.
      

	Figure 8: Elag grammar for genitive

Nevertheless, the word Ίντερνετ cannot be a plural; it is an error due to a false registration in the dictionary.

2.3.4. The accusative case
      Ambiguities between accusative and other cases may be found in all declensions. Here is a representative example representative for every genre:
      
         14. γίγαντα		N:Ams:Vms (giant)
      
         15. χώρα		N:Nfs:Afs:Vfs (country)
         
         16. παπούτσι	N:Nns:Ans:Vns (shoe)
         
      In order to define the accusative case, we described the sequences following certain prepositions. If the accusative grammar created initially is compared to the one below (figure 10) the following differences will be found:
- Some prepositions have been removed from the “if part” as they have been seen in texts before a nominative;
- The prepositional determiner has been added (e.g. στο :to the) in the “if part”; 
- The symbol % has been added after a number, because there may be a percentage;.
- Another adjective preceding a conjunction or punctuation has been added. (e.g. Έφυγε με ένα ΩΡΑΙΟ ΚΑΙ γρήγορο αυτοκίνητο: He left on a BEAUTIFUL AND quick car) 
- A personal pronoun that could precede the noun has been added. (e.g. Έφυγε με το ωραίο ΤΟΥ αμάξι: He left on HIS beautiful car)
- The prepositions in the “if part” have been also found before an abbreviation or punctuation sign (e.g. Απαγορεύεται να έρθετε με:……: You are not allowed to have with you:…).
	After applying the above graph, we obtained the results shown in figure 11. In particular, the word τομέα is recognized as an accusative, because it is following the prepositional determiner στον. 
3. Conclusions – Perspectives
      All grammars presented above, combined together, have been verified in electronic corpora. The ambiguities eliminated constitute only a small part of the ones existing in Modern Greek. Thus, more grammars should be created, which, along with the grammars presented above, will be applied to corpora as a united rule.
      Our aim is to have a Greek corpus semi-automatically labelled (every word will have only one morphological description) and this can only be attained with disambiguation grammars constantly tested in large corpora and updated electronic dictionaries. A semi-automatically labelled corpus could serve all computational linguistics applications (e.g. machine translation, automatic summarisation), which do not exist at a satisfactory level.

﻿Lexique-grammaire des verbes en grec moderne : 
Ambiguïtés formelles et analyse des textes

Kyriaki Ioannidou							    		      Tita Kyriacopoulou
Université Aristote de Thessalonique			       		  Université Paris-Est Marne-la-Vallée
kiroanni@auth.gr 							                                  tita@univ-mlv.fr 

Abstract
The subject of this paper is to put forward an overview of the research conducted in Greece, as regards the construction of the lexicon grammar of verbs and, more particularly, the table of classes. We present the problems observed in the table of classes that concern formal ambiguities. 
Keywords
lexicon-grammar, Greek verbs, table of classes, ambiguities observed in properties

Résumé
Nous présenterons ici un bilan du travail effectué sur les ressources du grec moderne concernant le lexique-grammaire des verbes et en particulier la table des classes que nous élaborons depuis quelques mois ainsi que les problèmes qui en découlent concernant les ambiguïtés formelles des propriétés.
Mots-clés
lexique-grammaire, verbes grecs, table des classes, ambiguïtés des propriétés

1. Introduction
   
   Les tables du lexique-grammaire grec sont en cours d’élaboration et elles constituent la principale source d’informations lexicales, syntaxiques et sémantiques de la langue. Il existe aujourd’hui 17 tables de verbes simples et 7 de noms prédicatifs. Leur développement a été initié il y a plusieurs années déjà par le LADL (Laboratoire d’Automatique Documentaire et Linguistique) (M. Gross 1975) et puis par le LTTL en Grèce (Laboratoire de Traduction et de Traitement Automatique du Langage). Le LTTL s’est fixé comme objectif l’élaboration de ressources linguistiques et d’outils pour l’analyse automatique des textes grecs. Ces outils doivent permettre, entre autres, d’effectuer l’analyse lexicale et syntaxique de textes à l’aide de dictionnaires électroniques. Le LTTL vise ainsi à intégrer technologie informatique et description linguistique. Dans ce but, ses chercheurs ont entrepris depuis 10 ans un programme de description formalisée de la langue grecque, à l’université Aristote de Thessalonique en collaboration avec l’université Paris Est Marne-la-Vallée, qui consiste en particulier à recenser et décrire le vocabulaire courant mais aussi technique, du point de vue morphologique et syntaxico-sémantique. 
   
   Il faut rappeler que la description du grec est faite par des chercheurs grecs qui ont été formés au cadre théorique du LADL (J.-P. Boons et al. 1976a ; J.-P. Boons et al. 1976b ; A. Guillet & C. Leclère 1992)  afin d’assurer la compatibilité des données linguistiques grecques (T. Kyriacopoulou 2005 ; A. Yannacopoulou 2005 ; V. Sfetsiou 2007 ; E. Fista et al. 2008). L’équipe de recherche grecque de la Section de Traduction du Département du Français, composée d’une dizaine de linguistes aujourd’hui, travaille déjà en traitement automatique des langues naturelles. 
   
   Afin de procéder à l’analyse syntaxique des textes grecs il faut intégrer les données du lexique-grammaire à un analyseur syntaxique ce qui nécessite la conversion des données à un format admis par l’analyseur. Nous nous sommes fortement inspirés de la conversion faite pour le français (B. Sagot & E. Tolone 2009) et nous  avons commencé par constituer la table des classes du grec. Nous nous sommes ainsi aperçus que de nombreux problèmes de cohérence et d’ambiguïté se posent. C’est l’objet de la présente étude. Nous présenterons, d’abord, un bilan du travail effectué depuis 10 ans et nous parlerons ensuite des ambiguïtés formelles des propriétés qui apparaissent lors de la génération automatique de la table des classes.

2. Un premier bilan des constructions verbales grecques

   L’approche théorique globale adoptée dans nos descriptions est celle de Z. Harris (1951) et le modèle adopté est celui du lexique-grammaire élaboré au LADL. Le lexique-grammaire est divisé en tables. Chaque table est caractérisée par une structure type qui définit l’emploi de ses entrées (dans notre cas, celui des verbes). En pratique, nous étudions les constructions de chaque verbe du dictionnaire morphologique et ensuite, nous réunissons dans une table les verbes qui partagent la même construction syntaxique. La structure commune de cette construction des verbes définit la table qui les regroupe. Mais la structure syntaxique ne suffit pas toujours pour définir des classes de verbes homogènes et pas trop volumineuses. De ce fait, nous avons ajouté d’autres propriétés sur la nature des compléments du verbe. Nous distinguons par exemple les verbes « à complétive » :

Ο Γιάννης ξέρει ότι η Μαρία τον αγαπάει
(Jean sait que Marie l'aime)

des verbes qui n’acceptent pas de complétives : 

Ο Γιάννης αγαπάει τη Μαρία
(Jean aime Marie)
   
   Un autre type de propriétés distributionnelles prises en compte est la caractérisation des substantifs (cf. Rapport LADL : Marques lexicales, syntaxiques, sémantiques dans le Delas V08/E, 1994). Par exemple, pour que la phrase Η Μαρία βαπτίζει την κόρη της (Marie baptise sa fille) soit reconnue (construction N0 V N1 où N1 est humain), il faut que le substantif κόρη (fille) ait le trait humain. Pour cette raison, nous avons ajouté des traits sémantiques dans nos dictionnaires électroniques. Pour le mot κόρη (fille) nous avons ainsi κόρη.N+hum (fille.N+hum). 
   
   Ce travail, qui constitue la base de la description syntaxique des verbes grecs, est un travail d’équipe qui a débuté il y a quelques années déjà mais dont les résultats sont encore incomplets. Nos recherches ont concerné les verbes transitifs non-locatifs (c’est-à-dire les tables 32GA, 32GD, 32GC, 32GCL, 32GCV, 32GH, 32GNM, 32GPL, 32GRA), les tables à complétives, 4G, 6G et 9G ainsi que les verbes transitifs locatifs (c’est-à-dire les tables 38GLS, 38GLD, 38GLH, 38GL0, 38GLR). Ces tables correspondent aux constructions suivantes :

32GA : N0 V N1		où N1 := apparition
32GD : N0 V N1		où N1 := disparition
32GC : N0 V N1		où N1 := concret
32GCL : N0 V N1		où N1 := partie du corps
32GCV : N0 V N1		où N0 Vsup N1 σε (en) Vn 
32GH : N0 V N1		où N1 := humain obligatoire
32GNM : N0 V N1		où *N1 être Vpp par N0
32GPL : N0 V N1		où N1 := pluriel obligatoire
32GRA : N0 V N1		où N0 κάνω+καθιστώ (rendre) N1 (E+πιο (plus)) Adj
4G : N0 V N1		où N0 := Pcomp
6G : N0 V N1		où N1 := Pcomp
9G : N0 V N1 σε N2		οù N1 := Pcomp
38GLS: N0 V N1 cor Loc N2 source 
38GLD: N0 V N1 cor Loc N2 dest 
38GLH: N0 V N1 cor hum Loc N2 dest
38GL0: N0 lieu V N1 cor
38GLR: N0 V N1 cor Loc N2 lieu
   
   À noter que les constructions intransitives ont été étudiées par A.-V. Pantazara (2003) et elles ne sont pas intégrés dans cette étude. 
   
   En grec moderne, il arrive souvent qu’un complément prépositionnel introduit par σε (à) soit équivalent à un complément direct au génitif. Par exemple, les deux phrases ci-dessous sont synonymes :

(1) Είπε ψέματα στη Μαρία (Prép = : σε)
(2) Είπε ψέματα της Μαρίας (Génitif)
(Il a dit des mensonges à Marie)
   
   Nous n’avons pas considéré cette propriété pour la classification des verbes. Par conséquent, c’est l’exemple (1) avec la préposition σε (à) qui sert de base à la classification et l’exemple (2) est considéré comme dérivé de (1) et répertorié comme tel dans la table.
   
   Toutes ces tables représentent 3969 emplois verbaux décrits et posent des problèmes de cohérence mais aussi de lacunes gênantes pour l’analyse automatique des textes. Ils seront discutés dans la suite. À noter cependant que contrairement aux tables du français les propriétés définitoires sont représentées dans les tables.

3. Application du lexique-grammaire aux corpus grecs

   Si l’on applique les tables du lexique-grammaire et les graphes paramétrés correspondants dans des corpus grecs, on ne reconnaît qu’une partie du sujet et des compléments du prédicat. Cela est dû au fait que la structure du sujet ou des compléments des prédicats dans le discours est assez complexe et ne peut pas être décrite exhaustivement dans le cadre d’une recherche concernant les propriétés syntaxico-sémantiques des prédicats et la construction des tables. En effet, les structures qui compliquent la description de la langue sont nombreuses : co-ordination, présence de déterminants complexes (M. Gross 1986) ou de phrases dans le groupe nominal, etc. 
   
   De plus, à part ce problème qui demande des recherches approfondies d'autres problèmes purement formels apparaissent et handicapent l'analyse des textes. Ils seront discutés en 3.2 après avoir présenté le corpus sur lequel nous nous appuyons pour faire nos tests.  

3.1 Ressources linguistiques

   Le corpus utilisé pour l’application des tables est un corpus journalistique de 5.0000.000 mots, que nous avons analysé via le système Unitex (S. Paumier 2008). Pour l’analyse typographique, nous avons utilisé la grammaire « Sentence » qui découpe le texte en phrases (T. Kyriacopoulou 2005). Par la suite, pour l’analyse lexicale, nous avons utilisé les dictionnaires morphologiques des mots simples et composés qui représentent 2 190 942 formes fléchies (T. Kyriacopoulou et al. 2006).
   
	À ce stade de l’analyse lexicale, nous avons appliqué les tables du lexique-grammaire dans le seul but de reconnaître des sous-structures syntaxiques et vérifier ainsi la validité de nos données. Cependant pour procéder à une analyse syntaxique du texte il faut convertir ces tables à un format « TAL » permettant ainsi son intégration dans un analyseur syntaxique. Dans ce but et en s’inspirant du travail fait pour le français (M. Constant & E. Tolone 2008), nous avons commencé à constituer la table des classes, c’est-à-dire une seule et unique table qui regroupera toutes les propriétés des prédicats. Rappelons que pour le français les tables des classes ont été développées par le LIGM (Laboratoire d’Informatique Gaspard Monge) pour chaque catégorie, et notamment des verbes, qui associent à chaque classe l’ensemble de ces propriétés définitoires. Ce travail a permis la conversion des tables en un format qui permet leur utilisation dans un analyseur syntaxique à grande échelle, l’analyseur FRMG (F. Thomasset & É. de La Clergerie. 2005). 
	
	Nous pouvons voir un extrait de la table des classes générée automatiquement par E. Tolone  à la figure 1. Chaque ligne de la table des classes correspond  à une propriété et chaque colonne correspond  à une table du lexique-grammaire. À l'intersection d'une ligne et d'une colonne, le signe o spécifie que cette propriété est étudiée dans la table correspondante. Cela veut dire que dans cette table, chaque entrée est caractérisée par la validité ou non de cette propriété. Si la propriété n'est pas du tout étudiée dans la table, il y a le symbole ?. Dans un futur proche et après correction des tables, nous préciserons dans la table des classes si les propriétés étudiées dans chaque table sont toujours valables (propriétés définitoires) ou si, par contre, leur validité dépend de chaque entrée de la table. En plus, nous indiquerons si les propriétés absentes d’une table (caractérisées par ?) sont vérifiées ou pas.

3.2 Ambiguïtés formelles

   Dans la table des classes, nous avons rencontré divers types d’ambiguïtés formelles. Tout d’abord certains intitulés qui expriment la même notion linguistique sont notés de façon différente. Par exemple Ν0 =: Nhum et N0 = : hum. À part les fautes de frappe, ces erreurs proviennent souvent du fait que la construction les tables s’étale sur plusieurs années et souvent elle est réalisée par des chercheurs différents. De plus, ces intitulés doivent être compatibles avec ceux utilisés dans les dictionnaires électroniques du type DELAGR (T. Kyriacopoulou 2005) qui eux aussi évoluent. Puisque les mises à jour des données sont rarement faite de façon systématique, des problèmes d’incohérence subsistent car les intitulés des propriétés du lexique-grammaire doivent s’accorder en général avec ceux des DELAGR utilisés à l’époque.  
   
   Dans d’autres cas, le même symbole peut avoir des interprétations diverses dans de différents intitulés. Par exemple le tiret (-) dans V-ικός symbolise le suffixe tandis que dans –hum symbolise l’absence du trait humain. Ces ambiguïtés seront discutées en détail dans la suite.

3.2.1. Plusieurs intitulés dans les tables des classes exprimant la même notion

   Comme nous avons déjà précisé, la construction des tables est faite par des linguistes grecs depuis une dizaine d’années et de façon manuelle. De ce fait, les intitulés des propriétés manquent de cohésion et cela apparaît immédiatement lors de la création de la table des classes qui est générée automatiquement (E. Tolone 2009) à partir des tables présentées à la section 2. En effet, la présence ou non des espaces, l’alternance des lettres majuscules et minuscules ou des caractères grecs et latins pour décrire les mêmes propriétés sont des fautes typographiques très fréquentes lors du traitement manuel des propriétés des prédicats. Les exemples suivants sont des intitulés qui se différencient par la présence de l’espace :

3.2.2. Un symbole correspondant à plusieurs significations

   En générant automatiquement la table des classes, nous avons remarqué que dans les tables du lexique-grammaire il existe des symboles qu’un analyseur syntaxique devrait interpréter différemment en fonction du contexte. Cela concerne en particulier les parenthèses, le tiret et le symbole de l’addition.
   
   Par exemple nous avons deux manières pour exprimer ce qui est optionnel en utilisant les parenthèses. 3.2.3. Ambiguïtés concernant les adjectifs

	Un autre type d’ambiguïté détectée concerne les  adjectifs. La notation A dans les dictionnaires électroniques du grec désigne l’adjectif en tant que catégorie grammaticale indépendamment de son étymologie. Par contre, il y a plusieurs notations utilisées dans les tables du lexique-grammaire pour spécifier les adjectifs dérivés des verbes V-adj comme par exemple V-ος et V-ικός qui expriment les adjectifs dérivés des verbes avec des terminaisons en –ος et –ικός. Il y a en plus la notation V :K qui se réfère aux participes passés qui jouent un rôle d’adjectif.
	
	L’utilisation de ces notations peuvent engendrer des problèmes de fusion : les participes passés peuvent être reconnus par V :K ainsi que par V-adj, V-ος et A ; les adjectifs dérivés de verbes qui se terminent par ος et ικός peuvent être reconnus par V-ος et V-ικός mais aussi par V-adj et A. 

4. Autres cas

   D’autres problèmes proviennent de la non cohérence de traits entre le dictionnaire électronique du type DELAGR et le lexique-grammaire. Par exemple, la notation Npl obl devrait reconnaître dans un texte des noms au pluriel non marqué morphologiquement comme πλήθος/foule mais elle ne reconnaît rien puisque le trait sémantique n’est pas explicité dans nos dictionnaires électroniques. 

5. Conclusion

   Pour compléter les tables du lexique-grammaire des verbes grecs, il reste beaucoup à faire. L’objectif de cette recherche est d’homogénéiser, corriger et compléter les données pour l’ensemble des tables du lexique-grammaire grec y compris les tables des noms prédicatifs. Une fois ces tables syntaxiques corrigées elles pourront être exploitées dans des analyseurs syntaxiques.

SUMMARY
This paper is about the creation of an electronic dictionary of sports terminology, which will be used by computers in natural language processing systems. This dictionary is based on the Greek version of a multilingual sports terminology database, which was processed within the framework of the Euradic project as part of the Technolangues project. The terms were first translated into Greek. The next step was to transfer the database entries to the electronic terminology dictionary including verbs, simple and compound nouns, adjectives, simple and compound adverbs, initialisms, frozen expressions. For the creation of this dictionary, the computational linguistics team of the Natural Language Processing Unit, Aristotle University of Thessaloniki, and Gaspard Monge Institute, University of Marne-la-Vallée used the same codification and automatic inflection programmes that were used for the creation of their general electronic dictionaries. The research was conducted by the said team, whose studies aim at a detailed and formalised description of Modern Greek, the final objective being the recognition of linguistic data by natural language processing systems.

The starting point of this study was the Euradic project as part of the Technolangues project. It was financed by the French Ministry of Economy, Finance and Industry.  The objective was the creation of a multilingual sports terminology database in the following languages: French, English, Arab, German and Greek. The full version will be soon made available on Internet for a fee.
Initially the Greek terms were translated from French. The next step was to generate the inflected forms of the Greek terms in order to use them in NLP systems. For this purpose we used the GenereFlexion programme.  The objective of this effort was to create an electronic dictionary - as complete as possible - of sports terms that would be applied in sports electronic corpora. This programme is a semi-automatic procedure combining the canonical forms of entries with the respective suffixes.

1.2	ΤΕRΜ TRANSLATION 
The translation of the terms was proved to be complicated, due to the significant lack of reliable terminology tools and multilingual sports dictionaries comprising a Greek section. Dictionaries (monolingual, bilingual and multilingual, general and specialised) and glossaries, regulatory texts, manuals, other terminology databases or banks were used as sources for the translation of the terms. Internet was widely used to get to these sources. Finding the regulatory texts was not always an easy task, as some sports are not very popular, especially in Greece (e.g. Nordic combined ski, bobsleigh, curling). Translation of winter sports terms proved also to be a difficult task, mainly because most of these sports are not even practised in Greece (or are practised at a very low scale). 
At the translation stage, we found many terms with established translation equivalent(s) in Modern Greek (e.g. ποδόσφαιρο/football). Among these terms there are also translation loans (ημιτελικός/semi-final), semantic loans (Ολυμπιακοί Αγώνες/Olympic Games), reborrowings (κάρτα/card), loans adapted to the inflectional system of Modern Greek with the addition of prefixes and/or suffixes (επινικελωμένο/nickel plated) and initialisms (FIG-Διεθνής Ομοσπονδία Γυμναστικής/FIG). The translation of the names of sports federations or organisations required extended research, as the official translation had to be found. 

However, we found terms with no translation equivalent(s) in Modern Greek. These terms were either composed of one word (e.g. bumper/αναστολέας) or of more than one word (e.g. εμπρόσθιος αναστολέας στερέωσης/front fastening brackets, έγγραφο για αθλητή που αποσύρεται λόγω σοβαρού τραυματισμού/”athlete withdrawn due to serious injury” document). In these cases we were obliged to create new terms by respecting the language structure. We must notice that a detailed analysis of the created neologisms is not possible in the framework of this study. 
2	TRANSFERRING THE DATA INTO THE GREEK ELECTRONIC DICTIONARY
2.1	ELECTRONIC MORPHOLOGICAL DICTIONARY OF MODERN GREEK
This study is part of the joint activities of the laboratories of computational linguistics of the Aristotle University of Thessaloniki1 and of the University of Marne-la-Vallée2, aiming to developing a complete and formalised description of the Modern Greek language. 
Formalised electronic dictionaries are needed for the natural language processing. By the term electronic dictionaries we mean the dictionaries created to be used by computers in natural language processing systems. The information contained in the electronic dictionaries should be as detailed and complete as possible. These dictionaries are constantly updated with new entries and their respective linguistic (morphological, syntactical and semantic) properties and grammars. Therefore, electronic dictionaries should include information about all inflected types of verbs, simple and compound nouns and adjectives. The simple words are sequences of letters comprised between two consecutive separators. The compound words are sequences including at least two simple words and at least one separator [1], [6]. In Greek there are the following separators [5]: the space (Ολυμπιακοί Αγώνες/Olympic Games), the hyphen (διαιτητής-κριτής/referee) and the apostrophe and space (κατ’ ευθεία πάσα/volley pass). 
The creation of the Greek electronic dictionaries was based on the DELA system, which was developed in LADL3 under the supervision of Maurice Gross. The DELA system includes DELAS, which is a dictionary of simple words, and DELAC, which is a dictionary of compound words. From DELAS and DELAC all inflected typed are automatically generated. They are included in DELASF and DELACF respectively. These dictionaries provide only morphological information4. 
Today, the dictionary of canonical forms of Modern Greek contains5:
	- 70 000 simple words;
	- 18 000 verbs;
	- 40 000 adjectives;
	- 16 000 simples and compounds adverbs;
	- 28 000 compound words;
	- 50 000 proper nouns;
	- 2 000 country names;
	- 1 000 simple and compound grammatical words.
To these entries there were added 940 simple words and 18 000 compound words of summer and winter Olympic sports terminology.	
2.2	RESTRUCTURING THE DATA 
At this stage the data were reclassified in a formalised way. Significant modifications were made to the initial classification. A distinction was made between simple and compound words. For instance, καλαθοσφαίριση/basketball was classified as a simple noun, whereas γραμμή οριοθέτησης/boundary line was classified as a compound noun. Both terms had initially been classified simply as nouns in the terminology database. The compound words are further divided in different categories, such as:
- A N, standing for the Adjective Noun type of compound words: αγωνιστικός (Α) χώρος (Ν) (wrestling area);
- N Ngen standing for the Noun Noun in genitive type of compound words: γραμμή (Ν) τέρματος (Ν) (goal line);
- N DET Ngen standing for Noun Determiner Noun in genitive type of compound words: σύνθεση (N) της (DET) ομάδας (N);
At the initial stage many terms had been classified as verbs, simply because the entry started with a verb. In many of these cases the new classification was different because the said verb was just the support verb. The element containing the semantic weight was the predicative noun [2], which means that the noun and not the verb should constitute an entry of the electronic dictionaries: κάνω βήματα/to travel, κάνω τάκλιν/to tackle. In this case, a full syntactic and semantic analysis will be obtained by the creation of a lexicon-grammar table [3]. The formalism of lexicon-grammar tables allows the detailed syntactico-semantic description of predicative nouns such as τάκλιν/tackle. This means that all the predicative nouns should be examined in relation with the support verbs which accompany them. 
In addition, at the translation stage, an acronym was given as a synonym for a term with no additional information: IAAF/I.A.A.F./Διεθνής Ένωση Ομοσπονδιών Στίβου/International Association of Athletics Federations. At the morphological description stage the distinction was made clear and acronyms were classified as such. 
2.3	GENEREFLEXION
GenereFlexion was used for the automatic inflection of all entries of the general electronic dictionaries and therefore for the automatic inflection of all sports terms. GenereFlexion is an automatic inflection programme, created by T. Kyriacopoulou and S. Mrabti [5]. It was developed in C, it launches in DOS or in UNIX and only plain text files (*.txt) can be processed with it. For the automatic generation of all inflected forms the programme uses three files. The first file contains the canonical forms together with a symbol to indicate the part of speech they belong to (N for nouns, A for adjectives, DET for articles, ADV for adverbs, CONJ for conjunctions and PREP for prepositions), the respective morphological code and the symbol “ in case the stress moves when the word is inflected. Specific filters can be used, e.g. S when the entry is used only in singular and P when the entry is used only in plural. In the case of compound words one symbol indicating the part of speech is used after each word of the compound lexical unit and a second one is used at the end of the sequence after the symbol used for the last word of the compound lexical unit, to indicate the part of speech of the compound word as a whole. In certain compound lexical units one of the consisting words is used only in a specific case: a specific filter is used to indicate the case (N for nominative, G for genitive, A for accusative and V for vocative) and, if necessary, the number of the word.

3	CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES
This study consisted in creating a multilingual sports terminology database, the Olympic Games being the reference point. This database was further processed to be integrated in electronic dictionaries. It was the first organised effort to include specialised terminology in our electronic dictionaries. Other specialities will follow to complete our dictionaries with technical terms. The second part of the research is placed in the framework of morphology. This means that further systematic description of the terms is needed at syntactical and semantic levels. More specifically, our objective is to create local grammars and lexicon-grammar tables [3], [7], in order to achieve the most complete possible recognition of electronic sport texts at all levels of analysis. The local grammars will concern the representation of common terms by finite state automata at the paradigmatic axis (e.g. football/basketball/volleyball ball). The lexicon-grammar tables will describe the predicative nouns and respective support verbs, providing all the necessary syntactical and semantic information.

CONSTRUCTION DU LEXIQUE LGLEX A PARTIR DES TABLES DU LEXIQUE-GRAMMAIRE DES VERBES DU GREC MODERNE

Résumé
Dans cet article, nous dressons un bilan du travail effectué sur les ressources du grec moderne concernant le Lexique-Grammaire des verbes1. Nous détaillons les propriétés définitoires de chaque table, ainsi que l'ensemble des changements effectués sur les intitulés de propriétés afin de les rendre cohérents. Grâce à l'élaboration de la table des classes, regroupant l'ensemble des propriétés, nous avons pu envisager la conversion des tables en un lexique syntaxique  : LGLex. Ce lexique, au format texte ou XML, est généré par l'outil LGExtract (Constant & Tolone, 2010). C'est un format directement exploitable dans les applications de Traitement Automatique des Langues (TAL).
Mots clés : Lexique-Grammaire, verbes du grec moderne, table des classes, LGLex, lexique syntaxique

1. Introduction
Depuis une dizaine d’années, un programme de description formalisée de la langue grecque pour l’analyse syntaxico-sémantique a permis de construire des tables du Lexique-Grammaire, en adoptant la méthodologie proposée par Gross (1975), Boons, Guillet et Leclère (1976a ; 1976b). À l’heure actuelle, nous disposons de 17 tables de verbes simples (Yannacopoulou, 2005 ; Fista, 2007 ; Kyriacopoulou, 2010 ; Voskaki, 2011), 7 de noms prédicatifs (Sfetsiou, 2007), 16 tables d'adverbes (semi-)figés (Voyatzi, 2006 ; Voyatzi & Kakoyianni-Doa, 2010) et 2 tables de noms composés (Kyriakopoulou, 2011)2. Dans le cadre de notre recherche, nous avons pris en compte les tables des prédicats verbaux, le même travail étant en cours pour les noms prédicatifs.
   Afin de procéder à l’analyse syntaxique des textes grecs, il faut intégrer les données du Lexique-Grammaire dans un analyseur syntaxique, ce qui nécessite la conversion des données dans un format exploitable en Traitement Automatique des Langues (TAL). Étant fortement inspirés des travaux déjà réalisés pour le français (Tolone, 2011), nous avons suivi la démarche suivante pour les prédicats verbaux : 
* collecter toutes les tables ;
* créer une table des classes, afin d'y faire figurer les propriétés définitoires décrites dans la littérature et de rendre cohérent l'ensemble des tables, comme cela a été fait pour le français (Tolone, 2009 ; Tolone et al., 2010) ; 
* créer un script interprétant chaque intitulé de la table des classes qui, exécuté par l'outil LGExtract (Constant & Tolone, 2010), permet de convertir les tables au format LGLex.
	Dans cet article, après la présentation des tables utilisées pour la construction du lexique syntaxique, nous allons lister les types de modifications effectuées lors de la mise en cohérence des propriétés. Par la suite, nous allons évoquer la création de la table des classes détaillée dans (Ioannidou & Kyriacopoulou, 2010) qui nous a permis d'ajouter les propriétés définitoires et d'assurer l’homogénéisation des intitulés des propriétés. Enfin, nous allons présenter le lexique syntaxique obtenu.

3. Modifications dans les tables
Pour procéder à la conversion du contenu de plusieurs tables en un seul fichier, il faut que l'ensemble des propriétés respecte les mêmes conventions de notation et de structure dans les différentes tables. De ce fait, nous avons effectué des modifications dans les tables à l’aide de la table des classes (voir section 4). Pour effectuer ces modifications, nous nous sommes basées sur la documentation des propriétés des verbes du français5. Cependant, nous avons pris en compte les particularités de la langue grecque, en essayant d’être le plus proche possible des notations françaises. Par exemple, l’encodage particulier qu’exige la langue grecque nous a conduit à ne pas garder les accents français dans les notations. Nous avons adopté donc les mêmes symboles en enlevant les accents (Prép → Prep pour préposition6). 
   Les changements effectués concernent toutes les colonnes des tables, sauf celles des exemples et des traductions qui n'ont pas encore été traitées. Ils peuvent être regroupés en cinq catégories : les modifications typographiques, les modifications au niveau de la structure des intitulés, l’ajout des informations lexicales, la suppression des colonnes qui contenaient des propriétés définitoires ou non pertinentes et les changements purement linguistiques.
   1. Parmi les erreurs typographiques, on rencontre des fautes dues à l’alternance de caractères grecs et latins, ou de caractères majuscules et minuscules (ppv → Ppv). D’autres modifications au niveau typographique concernent la présence ou non d'espaces (N0=:Nhum → N0 =: Nhum), le tronquage ou non d’une notation (disp → disparition), ou l’utilisation d’une notation différente (V-ος [-os] → V-adj, Sfx = ος [os]) (Ioannidou & Kyriacopoulou, 2010). Nous avons, de plus, utilisé la notation x-V où x correspond à un préfixe qui est ajouté aux verbes grecs (p.ex. εκ-V [ek-], συν-V [syn-], etc.) (Fista, 2007). Cette notation a une interprétation particulière lors de la conversion des tables au format LGLex. Si cette colonne vaut + pour une entrée verbale donnée, il faut spécifier cette nouvelle entrée qui correspond au verbe préfixé partageant les mêmes propriétés que le verbe non préfixé. Les changements au niveau typographique représentent la plus grande partie des changements effectués dans les tables (55%).
   2. À part les modifications qui concernent les symboles utilisés dans les intitulés des propriétés, nous avons effectué des changements qui concernent la structure des propriétés (utilisation de la virgule ou des symboles =:, =, utilisation d’une structure différente pour désigner un trait sémantique et un rôle thématique). Pour les notations qui existent seulement pour le grec, nous avons utilisé des structures proches de celles adoptées pour les autres intitulés, pour faciliter leur conversion au format LGLex. Par exemple, au lieu d'utiliser la notation Pfxεκ-[ek-]/source qu’on utilisait pour exprimer l’ajout du préfixe εκ qui donne la notion de source, nous avons noté la construction complète N0 εκ-V [ek-] N1 Loc N2 source qui est conforme aux conventions du Lexique-Grammaire. Dans le tableau ci-dessous, nous avons regroupé les différentes structures utilisées pour les intitulés dans les tables grecques7.    Plus précisément, concernant les cas, nous n’avons pas intégré l’information du cas à tous les noms, mais seulement si les règles générales de la langue ne sont pas respectées. Comme règles générales en grec moderne, nous considérons les suivantes :
a. toutes les prépositions introduisent un groupe nominal à l’accusatif (p.ex. από [apó=de] N0)
b. le sujet de la phrase (N0) se trouve toujours au nominatif 
c. le complément d’objet direct (N1) se trouve toujours à l’accusatif

		Il cache le pain avec la serviette / Il cache le pain à la serviette

c. Concernant les phrases complétives, nous avons utilisé la notation Pcomp0 pour exprimer qu’il s’agit d’une phrase complétive qui a la position syntaxique d’un sujet (N0) (Kyriacopoulou, 2005). Cette complétive est par ailleurs décrite explicitement dans d’autres colonnes de la table, comme l'ensemble des complétives, avec une notation du type Px où x désigne la conjonction qui introduit la phrase en question (Pνα, Pότι). Ceci diffère du français, où c'est le mode de la complétive qui est indiqué : subjonctif ou indicatif (Pind ou Psubj). La raison de cette différenciation est qu’il existe plusieurs conjonctions qui exigent l’indicatif (Pότι [óti=que], Pπως [pos=que], Pαν [an=si], Pπου [pou=que] et Pμήπως [mípos=si]). Dans un fichier à part, nous avons mis toutes les conjonctions utilisées dans une phrase complétive avec leur mode exigé (voir note 7). De plus, contrairement au français, c'est la complétive (et non l'infinitive) qui peut être contrôlée par le sujet N0 (ex. (7)). 

		(7)	Ο Πέτρος 	αμελεί 		να του τηλεφωνήσει
							N1 =: να (na=que) V0
			Pierre 		néglige 		de lui téléphoner

Enfin, les complétives peuvent être de plus nominalisées, c’est-à-dire introduites par το [to=le] ou το γεγονός [to gegonés=le fait].
d. Quant aux préfixes, ils ont une interprétation différente dans le lexique syntaxique, selon la structure de l’intitulé qui les contient (Fista, Kyriacopoulou, Martineau & Voskaki, 2008). Lorsque l'on a un intitulé contenant uniquement le préfixe avec la lettre V (ξε-V [kse-]), nous avons vu précédemment (cf. 3.1) qu'il fallait spécifier une nouvelle entrée constituée du préfixe suivi du verbe (sans tiret), qui accepte le même ensemble de propriétés et de transformations que le verbe sans préfixe. Lorsqu'ils sont utilisés dans une construction (p.ex. N0 εκ-V [ek-] N1 Loc N2 source), pour interpréter le prédicat, il faut ajouter le préfixe à l’entrée verbale dans cette construction uniquement. De plus, dans une construction, le préfixe peut être ajouté au participe passé du verbe (Vpp) avec une notation de la forme εκ-Vpp [ek-] (p.ex. N1 είμαι [eímai=être] ξε-Vpp [kse-]). Pour interpréter le prédicat dans une telle construction, il faut ajouter le préfixe au participe passé du verbe qui se trouve dans une colonne lexicale. Enfin, la notation X-V n'est pas exploitable, mais contient une information étymologique, indiquant que le verbe est formé d’un préfixe et d'un autre verbe.

4. Table des classes et lexique LGLex
Tout d'abord, toutes les propriétés définitoires vraies pour l'ensemble des entrées de chaque table (cf. section 2) ont été ajoutées. Au départ, la table des classes contenait 280 propriétés, incluant les différentes notations. À partir de la génération automatique de la table des classes, nous avons repéré les erreurs de notation pour les corriger directement dans les tables. La nouvelle table des classes générée contient 195 propriétés.
	Ensuite, le script d'extraction des verbes a été réalisé, comme pour le français (Tolone, 2011). Il spécifie toutes les opérations liées à chaque propriété devant être effectuées pour toutes les tables. Cela nous a permis de générer, à l'aide de LGExtract (Constant & Tolone, 2010), une première version du lexique LGLex des verbes grecs9, au format texte et XML. Ce lexique a vocation à décrire les tables avec les concepts manipulés par celles-ci, en un format directement exploitable dans les applications de TAL. L'une des utilisations informatiques possibles est la conversion en un autre format, comme cela a été fait pour le français (Tolone & Sagot, 2011). Dans sa version textuelle, une entrée de LGLex se présente comme suit :
* l'entrée commence par un identifiant indiquant sa catégorie, la table dont il provient et le numéro de l'entrée dans cette table (ID=catégorie_numTable_numEntrée) ;
* la section lexical-info indique les informations lexicales liées à l'entrée (le lemme et les prépositions associées à certains arguments) ;
* la section args décrit les distributions des différents arguments, avec éventuellement d'autres informations (traits sémantiques, mode et contrôle de la complétive, prépositions) ;
* la section all-constructions liste différentes constructions dans lesquelles l'entrée peut prendre part (soit nommées de façon complète avec tous les éléments dans l'ordre, soit des transformations à partir de construction de base) ;
* la section example illustre l'entrée.

5. Conclusion
L'objectif est d'homogénéiser, corriger et compléter les données pour l'ensemble des tables du Lexique-Grammaire du grec, y compris les tables des noms prédicatifs. Une fois ces tables syntaxiques corrigées, elles seront converties au format LGLex, afin d'être exploitables dans des analyseurs syntaxiques.
   Pour compléter les tables du lexique-grammaire des verbes grecs, il reste beaucoup à faire. En effet, il manque encore des entrées verbales à classifier (par exemple les verbes non transitifs) et la table des classes reste à coder avec les signes + et -. Les conventions de notation détaillées dans cet article seront à prendre en compte lors de la création de futures tables afin de garder l'ensemble cohérent. Puis, nous devons étendre notre travail aux noms prédicatifs, dont la modification des tables est déjà en cours. Ensuite, nous pourrons envisager la conversion du lexique LGLex au format Alexina, le format du lexique Lefff (Tolone & Sagot, 2011). Enfin, si l'on souhaite utiliser ce lexique syntaxique dans un analyseur syntaxique, il faudra adapter la méta-grammaire FRMG (Thomasset & de la Clergerie, 2005) du français à celle du grec, comme cela a été fait pour l'espagnol (Fernandez, 2010).

﻿COMPUTER-AIDED LEARNING OF TRANSITIVE NON-LOCATIVE CONSTRUCTIONS WITH A CONCRETE DIRECT OBJECT IN MODERN GREEK
Kyriaki Ioannidou, Eleni Tziafa, Rania Voskaki
Aristotle University of Thessaloniki (GREECE)
kiroanni@frl.auth.gr, etziafa@lit.auth.gr, rvoskaki@hotmail.com
Abstract
Formalised linguistic data integrated in Natural Language Processing (NLP) tools can prove useful in enhancing learning of Modern Greek as a foreign/second language. Our research focuses on transitive non-locative constructions with a concrete direct object (CDO). For example:

	(a)	Η νοσοκόμα κλείδωσε την πόρτα
		(The nurse locked the door)

Our proposal focuses on learning CDO by means of Unitex [1], a corpus processing system based on automata oriented technology. For this purpose, we have elaborated a lexicon-grammar table that includes verbs used in simple sentences containing CDO in Modern Greek [2]. Lexicon-grammar is a syntactic model [3], limited to the description of elementary sentences of a natural language. The description is given in the form of a binary matrix. In our research, this means that a verb has or not a certain property. Moreover, we created a parameterised finite state automaton that constitutes the symbolic representation of a lexicon-grammar table, enriched by finite state automata (FSA) that represent Greek noun phrases [4]. By noun phrases we mean the noun (noun substantive, pronoun or any other nominal) as a head of the noun phrase, along with all its modifiers [5] (p. 243); [6] (pp.127-128, 153-154). For example:

	(b) Η νοσοκόμα κλείδωσε την πόρτα του δωματίου του ασθενή
		(The nurse locked the door of the room of the patient)		(Literal translation)
		(The nurse locked the patient’s room)

We applied these linguistic resources on various authentic corpora (in different domains, such as science, economics, culture, etc.), in the context of Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL). We came up with a significant number of concordances, allowing the presentation of CDO within a bilateral context. The concordances are of great utility, since they provide the possibility to study the use of a given construction in large-scale corpora. Therefore, they contribute to heuristic methods of teaching. It is worth mentioning that the proposed method, dealing with CDO learning, is recommended as a complement to learners of levels B2, C1, C2 of the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR), set forth by the Council of Europe. Additionally, we have taken into account sample tests, past papers and guides to the examinations for the Certificate of Attainment in Modern Greek, organised by the Centre for the Greek Language.
The present research work is part of the research activities of the Laboratory of Translation & Language Processing in Aristotle University of Thessaloniki. Part of this research has been co-financed by the European Union (European Social Fund – ESF) and Greek national funds through the Operational Program "Education and Lifelong Learning"-"Investing in knowledge society" of the National Strategic Reference Framework (NSRF) 2007-2013. Research Funding Program: Heraclitus II. Investing in knowledge society through the European Social Fund1.
Keywords: Computer-aided learning, Modern Greek as a foreign/second language, concordances, NLP.

1 INTRODUCTION
The paper proposes a method of locating transitive non-locative constructions with a concrete direct object (CDO) as an aid to learners of Modern Greek, using Unitex [1], a corpus processing system based on automata oriented technology. For this purpose, we constructed the following linguistic resources: (a) a lexicon-grammar table describing Modern Greek verbs used in CDO simple sentences [2]; (b) a parameterised finite state automaton representing the syntactic and semantic properties included in the lexicon-grammar table; (c) a set of finite state automata (FSA), representing Greek noun phrases [4]. We applied those formalised linguistic resources on authentic electronic texts, including journalistic and educational discourse. We present the obtained results along with the utility of formalised linguistic resources, integrated in Natural Language Processing (NLP) tools, in the framework of enhancing learning of Modern Greek as a foreign/second language. More precisely, the proposed method for CDO learning could serve as a complement to learners for levels B2, C1, C2 of the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR), set forth by the Council of Europe. Moreover, we show how the corpus used meets the educational needs of Modern Greek learners, in the context of Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL).The above-mentioned matters will be successively presented. 
The current research has evolved around the Modern Greek language processing and it is integrated into a program for the development and the gradual completion of the Greek version of Unitex. Unitex is a platform developed for the analysis of texts in natural languages, using linguistic resources and tools. This work pertains to the field of NLP and is a product of the Laboratory of Translation and Language Processing (LTLP), at the School of French Language and Literature, in the Aristotle University of Thessaloniki. LTLP is a member of RELEX2, a network of laboratories in the domain of Computational Linguistics. 
2 LINGUISTIC DATA FORMALISATION
In order to formalise the linguistic data, we were based on the syntactic framework of the Transformational Grammar defined by Z. S. Harris [7]. The Harrisian theory initiated the Natural Languages formal analysis on the basis of a mathematical model. We have also followed the Lexicon-Grammar methodology framework developed by M. Gross [3] and elaborated at the Laboratory of Automatic Documentation and Linguistics (LADL). According to the Lexicon-Grammar methodology elementary sentences are taken as the minimal unit for the study of meaning and syntax. The predicate is the core of an elementary sentence; in our study, this core is the verb. Each verb has a specific set of arguments (i.e. subject and complements), depending on its meaning. Hence, a lexicon-grammar table incorporates syntactic properties, both distributional and transformational, systematically described. The systematic description consists of matrices whose rows are verbs; columns are syntactic properties into which verbs may enter or not. Matrices are binary: a plus sign appears at the intersection of a given row and a given column, when the verb in the row enters the structure represented in the given column; a minus sign appears in the opposite case. 
2.1 Lexicon-grammar table for transitive non-locative constructions with a concrete direct object in Modern Greek
The first step was to list the Modern Greek verbs used in CDO, in order to describe their syntactic properties in a lexicon-grammar table. We came up with 300 verbs, presenting the following basic structure: N0 V N1conc3. For example:
		(1a)	Οι οπαδοί ξήλωσαν τις κερκίδες
			(The sports fans banged up the bleachers)

In our research, for the term 'concrete object', we adopted the definition given in traditional grammars, considering as 'concrete nouns' a noun category referring to physical world, in opposition to 'abstract nouns' (i.e. 1a). However, that criterion is not always pertinent [8]. Therefore, we also considered as 'concrete objects', nouns such as πληγή (wound), illustrated in the following example:
		(2a)	Το φάρμακο επούλωσε την πληγή
			(The medicine healed the wound)

Furthermore, we analysed and described the listed verbs, by indicating for each verb its distributional and transformational properties, as shown in the following lexicon-grammar table.

2.2 Parameterised finite state automaton
In order to incorporate the description provided by the lexicon-grammar table in Unitex, we had to elaborate a parameterized FSA. Parameterized FSA are meta-graphs that allow the automatic generation of a set of graphs on the basis of a lexicon-grammar table. A FSA that describes the possible constructions is constructed manually. That FSA refers to the columns of the lexicon-grammar table in the form of parameters or variables. Afterwards, for each line of the table a copy of this graph is constructed, where the variables are replaced with the contents of the cell at the intersection of line and the column that corresponds to the variable. If a cell of the table contains the + sign, the corresponding variable is replaced by <E>. If the cell contains the - sign, the box containing the corresponding variable is removed, interrupting the paths through that box. In all other cases the variable is replaced by the contents of the cell. 

	Figure 2: Extract of the parameterised automaton for CDO in Modern Greek
2.3 Finite state automata for Greek noun phrases
Τhe parameterised FSA presented above has been enriched with a set of FSA describing complex noun phrases in object position. Within the scope of our research, by noun phrases we mean the noun (noun substantive, pronoun or any other nominal) as a lexical head of the noun phrase, along with its modifiers [5] (pp. 243); [6] (pp.127-128, 153-154)4. For example: 
		(1a)	Οι οπαδοί ξήλωσαν τις (/όλες τις) κερκίδες και τα κιγκλιδώματα
			[The sports fans banged up the (/all the) bleachers and the railing]

		(2b)	Το φάρμακο επούλωσε την πληγή του ασθενή
			(The medicine healed the wound of the patient)	(Literal translation)
			(The medicine healed the patient's wound)

Therefore, on the basis of the CDO simple phrases cited in section 2.1, we present in the above-mentioned phrases possible complex noun phrases in object position (marked in bold) that are successfully recognized after the application of the elaborated set of FSA. 
In NLP, the task of noun phrase recognition by means of grammars is referred to as noun phrase chunking. Chunking, also known as light parsing, is a method for parsing natural language sentences into partial syntactic structures. This kind of analysis identifies the constituents of a sentence (noun groups, verbs, verb groups, etc.), but does not specify neither their internal structure nor their role in the main sentence.
However, we propose the use of parameterized FSA combined with chunking graphs, in order to take advantage of every type of FSA. On the one hand, the use of parameterised FSA allows us to recognize certain syntactic terms, direct objects in our study, as they are linked to a lexicon-grammar table. On the other hand, chunking automata allow the recognition of a more complex structure of syntactic terms.
More specifically, in the chunking grammars created, we described structures both of base noun phrases and of maximal-length noun phrases [12], [13], [14]. Base noun phrases are non-recursive noun phrases, that is, nouns (pronouns or other nominals) that are determined by determiners, adverbs or other words; however, they are not determined by other noun phrases. As Ramshaw & Marcus [12] explain, “these chunks basically select noun phrases that contain no nested noun phrases”. For example:
	(3)	Αποστείρωσε το μικρό μπουκάλι
	(He sterilised the small bottle)

On the contrary, maximal-length noun phrases are base noun phrases, as described above, which are modified by another base noun phrase. As far as maximal-length noun phrases are concerned, the structures included in our descriptions are the following:
a. The first noun phrase in object position (the house) is in accusative case, while the second noun phrase that follows (of their employer) is in genitive case:
       (4)	Οι εργάτες έκαψαν το σπίτι του εργοδότη τους
    (The workers burnt the house of their employer)	(Literal translation)
       (The workers burnt their employer’s house)
b. The first (the doll) and the second noun phrase (its clothes) in object position are linked by coordination:
       (5)	Πλένω την κούκλα και τα ρούχα της
    (I am washing the doll and its clothes)

In our research, we have not taken into account the following cases:
a. Base noun phrases modified by prepositional phrases; according to Abney [15]: “noun phrases constitute chunks, but prepositional phrases are considered to belong to a level above the chunk level and are not marked as chunks”. For example:
	(6)	Μετράω τα σπίτια στο λόφο
	(I am counting the houses on the hill)

b. Base noun phrases modified by relative clauses; the recognition of such phrases should be achieved during deep parsing, as they should be syntactically analysed. For example:
	(7)	Πληκτρολόγησε το κείμενο που της έδωσαν
	(She typed the text that they gave her)

We constructed and used 382 FSA graphs in total, in order to recognize all possible structures of noun phrases as direct objects. These graphs are recalled by two super-graphs, uniting all graphs concerning nominative and accusative case respectively. The two super-graphs are integrated in the parameterised FSA in the appropriate positions, as described in section 2.2 in this paper. The high number of graphs is due to the presence of cases in Modern Greek. More specifically, in order to avoid morphological ambiguities, we gave a detailed description as far as morphological information is concerned, so as to ensure agreement in case, number and genre for all constituents of the noun phrase. In Fig. 3, we can see a FSA recognizing all possible combinations of adjectives and adverbs, when determining a noun in accusative case.


	Figure 3: Finite state automaton recognizing all possible combinations of adjectives and 		adverbs, when determining a noun in accusative case

3 IMPLEMENTATION AND RESULTS 
Our next step was to apply the enriched FSA (consisting of the parameterized FSA, presented in section 2.2 and the two super-graphs described in section 2.3) on an authentic corpus. We have to note that Modern Greek is a less resourced language; moreover, the corpora available as full texts are quite a few. The current research is based on the Modern Greek Texts Corpus, which is maintained by the Portal for the Greek Language5. It comprises of 7,000,000 words and it consists of three sub-corpora: a text corpus compiled by the newspaper Ta Nea, a text corpus compiled by the newspaper Makedonia and a text corpus formed by the school books of secondary education, as provided by the Pedagogical Institute. The texts included belong either to journalistic or educational discourse. The journalistic corpus comprises of more than 5,000,000 words (3,000,000 derived from the newspaper Makedonia and 2,000,000 words by the newspaper Ta Nea). The texts are organised in thematic units and text types. The pedagogical corpus comprises of 2,000,000 words and the texts are derived of students’ and teachers’ books in secondary education. The texts are also organised in thematic units and text types6. 
The small size of the corpus is justified as for the study of grammatical structures a larger sample of texts is not considered necessary [16] (pp. 91); [17] (pp. 32). Moreover, smaller text corpora may provide adequate information when the frequency of the phenomenon under study, such as noun phrases, is relatively high [18].
Initially, we preprocessed the corpus, by means of Unitex. The corpus preprocess included the following operations: nominalization of separators, identification of tokens, normalization of non-ambiguous forms, splitting into sentences and application of electronic dictionaries. Then, we applied the enriched parameterized FSA. The corpus processing systems search in texts concrete morphosyntactic structures, which are treated and presented in the form of concordance tables. Concordancers generate a list, including all the words of a given text, chosen on the basis of the dictionary, retrieved alphabetically, and presenting each word within the bilateral context. Concordances are of great utility, because they allow studying the use of a given word in its context. Moreover, they provide information about the sort of context, narrow or broad, where the word occurs.

In order to assess the quality of the elaborated FSA (both the parameterised FSA and the super-graphs), two basic evaluation measures should be used: precision and recall. In the context of the present study, precision could be defined as the ratio of CDO over the total number of CDO retrieved by the corpus processing system. Recall could be the ratio of CDO retrieved the corpus processing system over the total number of CDO in the corpus. For the precision percentage, it would be essential to calculate the CDO retrieved from the text, while for the recall percentage it would be required to locate the exact number of correct CDO included in the text. For this purpose, a syntactically tagged corpus is necessary. However, there are no tagged corpora available in Modern Greek, at least not freely available. Therefore, it has not been possible, in the framework of this study, to assess the quality of the FSA we created by recall and precision. Certainly, it is among the perspectives that we will endeavour to fulfill through future research.
4 ENHANCING LEARNING OF MODERN GREEK AS A FOREIGN SECOND LANGUAGE
In the framework of Information Communications Technology in education (ICT), formalised linguistic data integrated in NLP tools can prove useful in enhancing learning of Modern Greek as a foreign/second language. Awareness is very important during foreign language learning, but awareness without input is not sufficient [19]. Therefore, the use of NLP tools is more than necessary.
More precisely, we propose learning of CDO by means of Unitex7, because it is an open-source system, operating for various natural languages; among others for Modern Greek. Linguistic resources (i.e. electronic dictionaries) as well as basic grammars in FSA form (i.e. sentence segmentation) are integrated in the system. Currently, the open-source version contains 30% of the elaborated (by LTLP) electronic dictionaries, a sample journalistic corpus and the basic grammars. However, we intend to incorporate more linguistic data in the open-source version. 
The advantage of the corpus processing system is that the user has the possibility to add any corpus available, any corpus that fulfils the educational needs of his foreign language learning class. The user should only preprocess the added corpus, convert it in unicode format and apply the electronic dictionaries. This procedure provides a normalised corpus, segmented in sentences, with recognized lexical units. Every electronic dictionary elaborated to date presents a small percentage of unrecognized lexical units (less than 10% in journalistic corpus). By applying linguistic data, formalised as FSA, the user comes up with a significant number of concordances. The concordances are of great utility, since they provide the possibility to study the use of a given construction in authentic and/or large-scale corpora. Therefore, they contribute to heuristic methods of teaching [2] (pp.212-222).
4.1 Content and Language Integrated Learning
In the current research we used a journalistic and an educational discourse corpus. Both of them are classified in thematic units and text types [20]. More precisely, the journalistic corpus contains the following thematic units: (i) economics, (ii) book presentation, (iii) visual arts, (iv) arts and culture. In addition, it consists of the text types as follows: (i) sports news, (ii) reportage, (iii) gastronomy, (iv) interview, (v) advertisements, (vi) science news, and (vii) artistic reviews. As for the educational corpus it mainly comprises of various thematic units, such as: (i) biology, (ii) history, (iii) translated Ancient Greek texts, (iv) physics, (v) chemistry, (vi) economics theory, (vii) Modern Greek learning, (viii) religion and (ix) mathematics. In the text types’ classification, it is divided in: (i) biographies, (ii) curriculum vitae, (iii) short stories, (iv) stories, (v) forecast, (vi) dialogs, (vii) interviews, (viii) advertisements, (ix) research and (x) e-mail. 
The classification shows that it is caracterised by a significant variety of different thematic fields, which principally pertain to curricular subjects. Therefore, it can contribute to the cross-cultural awareness of the Modern Greek learner through the medium of a language other than that normally used; we are especially referring to the educational corpus. Moreover, it can be effective, as a language learning complement, for the literacy of all levels of students, from teenagers (often more familiar with terminology included in curricular subjects) to adults. As it was recently set forth by researchers, in charge of the design of the exams for the Attainment in Modern Greek [21], CLIL is also taken into consideration while preparing exams.
4.2 Common European Framework of Reference for Languages 
Teaching and/or learning of CDO in Modern Greek by means of Unitex and the formalised linguistic data described in the present study could serve as a complement to learners of B2, C1, C2 levels of the CEFR, set forth by the Council of Europe. In order to obtain a Certificate of Foreign Language Attainment, the candidate is examined, as known, on a set of skills. One of them is the examination of grammar accuracy. As for the C2 level, the use of complex structures is strongly required. For C1 level, the equivalent requirements are almost the same, allowing a few structural errors. Finally, in B2 level circumstantial structural oversight are allowed [22] (pp. 171). CDO in Modern Greek present a high degree of complexity, mainly in the case of complex noun phrases in object position, as they are presented in 2.3 of the present paper. 
We have also taken into account sample tests, past papers and the guide [23] to the examinations for the Certificate of Attainment in Modern Greek, organised by the Centre for the Greek Language. More precisely, we studied past papers used for B2, C1 and C2 levels the last five years. Activities requiring the right order of a given text have been observed in those recent past papers. Those types of activity involve awareness of the language structures. 
5 CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES
In this paper, we have described the procedure of recognition of CDO in Modern Greek by means of a corpus processing system based on automata oriented technology (Unitex). A lexicon-grammar table, a parameterised FSA and a set of FSA for Greek noun phrases have been utilised to track CDO in authentic corpora. We have also focused on the utility of the obtained concordances in enhancing learning of Modern Greek as a foreign/second language for learners of levels B2, C1, C2 of the CEFR.
Perspectives that we will endeavour to fulfil through future research are as follows: enrichment of the existing corpora; improvement of the FSA, in order to eliminate, as much as possible, ambiguities; tagging syntactically part of the corpus, so as to be able to evaluate the quality of the obtained results, by recall and precision. In addition, expanding recognition of other types of direct objects within the transitive non locatives constructions in Modern Greek –such as ‘human object’, ‘body part object’, ‘obligatory plural object’– would be useful in computer-aided learning of Modern Greek as a foreign/second language.
